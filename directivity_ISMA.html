The paper in one minute

The central idea is to replace dense spherical maps with a small set of physically readable directional lobes that remain useful for comparison, retrieval, and corpus-level analysis.

Problem

Rich maps are hard to browse

Spherical-harmonic directivity maps preserve physical detail, but they are cumbersome for repeated comparison, clustering, and interactive exploration across large datasets.

Model

Energy as a distribution on S2

For each frequency band, the directional energy pattern is sampled on the sphere and normalized to unit mass, so that the model captures angular radiation rather than overall level.

Descriptor

vMF components as radiating lobes

Each component has a mean direction, a concentration, and a weight. The target similarity controls how many lobes are retained for each band.

Interpreting the model

Frequency-aware

At lower frequencies, broad radiation can often be represented by a small number of components. In the upper bands, the model allocates more components to secondary lobes and fine angular structure.

The 70% setting is emphasized because it provides a strong balance between compactness and preservation of inter-instrument distance geometry.

Main results

Why the vMF view is useful

It turns a dense source-radiation map into an interpretable directional-statistical object: component directions describe where energy is radiated, concentrations describe lobe sharpness, and weights describe relative prominence.

What remains open

Future extensions can add continuity constraints across frequency, explicit playing-condition dependence, and learning-oriented descriptors for musical acoustics and spatial audio workflows.
